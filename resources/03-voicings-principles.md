# Piano Voicings - Comprehensive Guide

## What are Voicings?

**Voicing** refers to how the notes of a chord are spread out. Each note is sometimes called a "voice". Good voicings depend on understanding register, spacing, and voice movement.

## Register Guidelines

### Low Register (up to C below middle C)
- **Space notes far apart** - close intervals sound muddy
- Why? Low notes produce abundant overtones (harmonic series)
- **Avoid**: 7ths, 3rds, and close intervals in very low register
- Exception: If you want muddy sound for effect, use sparingly

### Mid Register (C below middle C to F above)
- **Most resonant area** - rich sound without muddiness
- Notes can be fairly close together
- Intervals of 4ths, 3rds, major 2nds work well
- Limit minor 2nds to 1-2 maximum for bite
- **This is where left hand is centered**

### Upper Register (above middle C to F+octave+4th)
- Notes still strong but less resonant
- Can use very close intervals (many minor 2nds, clusters)
- Higher chord extensions work without worry

### Highest Register (above F+octave+4th)
- Notes get increasingly thin
- Close clusters possible but weak unless supported by lower notes
- **Double the lead an octave below** for strength

## Strength of Intervals (strongest → weakest)
1. Octave (strongest)
2. Perfect 5th
3. Perfect 4th
4. 3rd
5. 2nd (weakest)

## Essential Notes vs. Omissions

### Must Include:
- **3rd (or 4th in sus)** - defines major/minor quality
- **7th** - defines extended harmony (except in triads/6th chords)

### Can Omit:
- **Root** - especially when playing with bass player
- **5th** - especially in dominant 13th chords (weakens the 13th)

### Avoid Doubling:
- Root in low/medium register (sounds too grounded)
- Major 3rd in medium register (too syrupy)
- Minor 3rd doubling is more acceptable

## Problematic Intervals

### Minor 9th Issues
Avoid minor 9th between two voices (not necessarily adjacent) in these cases:
- 4th a m9 above major 3rd
- Root in melody with major 7th a m9 below
- Minor 7th in melody with 13th a m9 below
- Major 3rd in melody with #9 a m9 below
- 5th in melody with #4 a m9 below

**Rule**: Problem arises when basic chord tones are in the lead with higher interval note below

### Other Avoid Combinations:
- Natural 9th with altered 9ths
- Natural 5th with #5 (but ♭5 can coexist with natural 5)
- Major 7th and minor 7th together

## Dominant 13th Chords Special Rules
- Usually avoid 5th a major 9th below
- Better: use ♭5, double the 13th, or omit 5th entirely
- Don't use two 3rds (they overpower other notes)

## Voice Movement
- Pay attention to **inner voice movement**
- In legato passages: each voice should move by steps (tone/semitone) rather than leaps
- Creates smooth, connected sound

## Drop Voicings (Arranging Terms)

**Close Position**: C E G B (stacked closely)

**Open Position I (Drop 2)**: Drop 2nd note from top down an octave
- C E G B → G C E B

**Open Position II (Drop 3)**: Drop 3rd note from top down an octave  
- C E G B → E G C B

**Five-Note Voicings**: Double the lead
- B C E G B (Shearing/Glenn Miller sound)
- Can also be opened with drop voicings

## Quartal Harmony
- Chords built from 4th intervals (not all perfect 4ths)
- Modern, ambiguous sound
- Alternative to traditional tertian (3rd-based) harmony

## Classical Resolution Rule
- 7th of dominant chord should resolve down a semitone to 3rd of next chord
- Can be ignored in jazz/modern contexts
- Be aware of the "pull" even when breaking the rule

## Learning Strategy
1. Get voicing ideas from books and recordings
2. When you find something you like, play it in all keys
3. Analyze: chord type, extensions/alterations, voicing structure
4. Remember: learning voicings = foundation for arranging and composition
