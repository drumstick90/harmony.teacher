# Non-Chord Tones - Advanced Concepts

Source: Introduction to Classical Tonal Harmony - Luis Robles

## 1. Fundamental Principle

**CRITICAL**: Any non-chord tone that is not properly used will be interpreted by the ear as a **chord tone**.

- Faulty use distorts the harmony
- Can even change the perceived harmonic function
- Example: Incorrectly used "passing tone" becomes part of the chord (F triad → F7)

## 2. Diatonic vs. Chromatic Non-Chord Tones

### Diatonic (Most Common)
- Belong to the scale of current key
- Standard practice

### Chromatic
- **Not from current scale**
- Produce special coloristic effects
- Can evoke different tonalities
- Often used to "announce" modulations before they occur
- Example: F♯ as chromatic neighbor in C major → prepares modulation to G major

**Important**: Chromaticism NOT applicable to suspensions and pedal tones

## 3. Simultaneous Non-Chord Tones

When multiple voices use non-chord tones simultaneously:
- Must form a coherent sonority (usually a chord)
- Creates "second category" or "fictitious" chords
- Chord symbols for these are placed in brackets

### Detection Clues:
1. **Absence of harmonic function** - doesn't fit in basic harmonic system
2. **Short duration** - shorter than normal harmonic rhythm
3. **Unusual note behavior** - e.g., descending leading tone
4. Often forms **secondary dominant** of following chord

## 4. Non-Chord Tones Applied to Other Non-Chord Tones

Relatively rare but used by composers:
- Example: Passing notes embellished with neighbor tones
- Creates layered ornamentation

## 5. THE SUSPENSION (Important!)

### Definition
An **appoggiatura** tied to a previous chord tone

### Characteristics:
- Placed at interval of **2nd** before resolution note
- **Preceded by same note** (preparation) - usually tied, always a chord tone
- Placed on **strong metrical accent** at beginning of harmonic change
- **Descending suspensions** (resolve downward) much more common than ascending

### Three Parts:
1. **Preparation** - chord tone, often tied
2. **Suspension** - non-chord tone on strong beat
3. **Resolution** - moves by step (usually down) to chord tone

### Unique Aspects:
- Unlike other non-chord tones, **often in inner voices**
- Creates interesting dissonance against actual chord tones
- Can be indicated in figured bass notation

## 6. ESCAPE TONES

"Reversed appoggiatura" - rather uncommon

### Characteristics:
- Placed on **metrically weak moment**
- **Follows chord tone at interval of 2nd** (stepwise approach)
- **Followed by leap**, usually in opposite direction to previous stepwise motion

## 7. THE PEDAL POINT (Fundamentally Different!)

### Definition
A note that remains unchanged while other voices play different harmonies, even foreign to that note.

**Key Insight**: The pedal IS the real chord tone; notes around it are the non-chord tones!

### Characteristics:

**Dominant Pedal**:
- Prolongs tension of dominant
- Usually in middle of piece
- Extends tensional effect

**Tonic Pedal**:
- Prolongs relaxation of tonic
- Usually at beginning or end
- Extends stability

### Rules:
- Pedal always begins and ends with the chord of which it's the root
- Almost exclusively on **Dominant or Tonic** roots
- Most often in bass (but can be in other voices)
- Often encountered in arpeggiated form in piano music

### Function:
- Retains harmony of which it is the root note
- Creates sense of grounding or anticipation depending on function

---

## Summary: Practical Applications

1. **Proper usage is critical** - incorrect non-chord tones change perceived harmony
2. **Chromatic non-chord tones** can prepare modulations and add color
3. **Suspensions** create rich dissonances, especially effective in inner voices
4. **Pedal points** (especially dominant pedal) build sustained tension or stability
5. Non-chord tones can be simultaneously combined if they form coherent sonorities
